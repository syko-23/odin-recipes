Lasagna Recipe

Description

There’s something deeply satisfying about a homemade lasagna—the kind that takes a lazy Sunday afternoon to assemble and fills the whole house with the rich, garlicky aroma of slow-simmered meat sauce. Unlike the frozen or cafeteria versions that can turn out watery or rubbery, a proper homemade lasagna is a labor of love, built in layers of texture and flavor. It starts with a hearty ragù: ground beef and Italian sausage browned until deeply caramelized, then gently simmered for at least an hour with crushed tomatoes, tomato paste, onion, garlic, fresh basil, and a splash of red wine. Some cooks add a hidden carrot or celery for sweetness, or a pinch of nutmeg to echo the old Northern Italian tradition. While the sauce burbles on the stove, a creamy béchamel or a simple ricotta-spinach mixture is prepared—each family has its own allegiance. The ricotta version, whipped smooth with an egg, grated Parmesan, parsley, and a little salt, provides that dreamy, cloud-like layer between the pasta.

The assembly is where patience pays off. Start with a thin slick of sauce on the bottom of a deep baking dish to prevent sticking, then lay down no-boil lasagna noodles (or fresh pasta sheets, if you’re feeling ambitious). Next comes a generous smear of ricotta mixture, a ladleful of meat sauce, and a shower of shredded low-moisture mozzarella. Repeat these layers two or three times, finishing with a final blanket of sauce and an extravagant topping of mozzarella and Parmesan. The key is not to skimp on the sauce between layers, or the pasta will come out dry and tough. Cover tightly with foil and bake at 375°F for about 40 minutes, allowing the flavors to meld and the noodles to absorb all the tomatoey juices. Then remove the foil for the last 15 to 20 minutes, letting the top turn into a blistered, golden-brown cheese crust that crackles when you tap it.

The hardest part comes after the lasagna leaves the oven: letting it rest. Ten minutes is the absolute minimum—fifteen is better. This brief wait allows the molten layers to set so that when you cut into it, the squares hold their shape instead of turning into a saucy landslide. Serve it with a simple side salad dressed in lemon and olive oil, and maybe some crusty bread to swipe up any stray bits of cheese from the plate. Leftovers, if there are any, are almost better the next day, when the flavors have had overnight to deepen. A homemade lasagna isn’t just dinner; it’s a small celebration of time and care—a dish that says someone in the kitchen really wanted to feed you well.

Ingredients

For The Meat Sauce

  • 30g Ground Beef
  • 50g Crushed Tomatoes
  • 1 Minced Onion
  • 1 Minced Garlic
  • Chopped Fresh Basil
  • Salt and Pepper
  • Drizzle of Olive Oil

For The Ricotta Layer

  • 35g Ricotta Cheese
  • 1tbsp Parmesan Cheese
  • 1tsp Chopped Parsley
  • Yolk of 1 Egg

For the Noodles & Cheese

  • No-boil Lasagna Noodles
  • Shredded Mozzarella
